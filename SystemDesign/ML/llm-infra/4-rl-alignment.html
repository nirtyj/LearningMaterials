RL-Based Alignment

From human preferences to verifiable rewards — making models helpful, harmless, and honest through reinforcement learning.

4.1

The Alignment Problem

Why supervised fine-tuning alone is not enough

Pre-training produces a powerful language model, and SFT teaches it to follow instructions in a desired format. But SFT has a fundamental limitation: it teaches the model to imitate demonstrations, not to optimize for outcomes. When the goal is nuanced — be helpful but not harmful, be accurate but admit uncertainty — imitation learning hits a ceiling. Reinforcement learning bridges this gap by letting the model explore and learn from evaluative feedback rather than teacher forcing.

LIMITATION

SFT Teaches Format, Not Judgment

SFT optimizes cross-entropy against reference answers. It cannot distinguish a good answer from a great one — both get the same gradient if they match the reference. The model learns surface patterns (tone, structure, verbosity) without learning which answers humans actually prefer.

PROBLEM

Reward Hacking

When optimized against a reward signal, models learn to exploit the reward function rather than genuinely improve. Examples: producing longer answers because the reward model correlates length with quality, or hedging excessively because the RM scores cautious refusals higher. Managing reward hacking is a central challenge in RL-based alignment.

The Alignment Tax

Aligned models may sacrifice some raw capability for safety and helpfulness. A model steered away from generating harmful content may also become less creative or less willing to engage with edge cases. The goal of modern alignment is to minimize this tax — maximize helpfulness while maintaining safety guardrails.

Why RL Is Needed — Non-Differentiable Objectives

  • Human preference: "Which response do you prefer?" is a ranking, not a continuous gradient signal
  • Code correctness: Code either passes tests or it doesn't — binary, non-differentiable
  • Factual accuracy: A claim is true or false; partial credit is hard to express as cross-entropy loss
  • Safety compliance: Whether a response violates a policy is a discrete judgment call
  • Tool use success: Did the model call the right API with the right parameters? Pass/fail.

RL turns these binary or ordinal signals into gradient updates the model can learn from, enabling optimization of objectives that SFT cannot directly address.

4.2

RLHF / PPO

The classic InstructGPT pipeline that started it all

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) was popularized by the InstructGPT paper (2022) and became the standard alignment recipe for ChatGPT, Claude, and other frontier models. The pipeline has three stages: supervised fine-tuning, reward model training, and PPO-based policy optimization.

STAGE 2

Reward Model (RM)

Trained on human preference pairs: given prompt x and two responses y_w (preferred) and y_l (rejected), the RM learns to assign a higher scalar reward to the preferred response. The loss is the Bradley-Terry pairwise ranking objective:

L(RM) = -log( sigma( r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l) ) )

Typically initialized from the same SFT model with a scalar head replacing the LM head. Quality of the reward model is the ceiling for alignment quality — a noisy RM will teach the policy to exploit noise.

STAGE 3

PPO — Proximal Policy Optimization

PPO is a policy gradient algorithm with a clipped surrogate objective that prevents catastrophically large parameter updates:

L(PPO) = -E[ min( r_t * A_t, clip(r_t, 1-eps, 1+eps) * A_t ) ]

where r_t = pi_new / pi_old is the probability ratio and A_t is the advantage estimate.

KL Penalty

  • Purpose: Constrains the trained policy from drifting too far from the SFT model (reference policy)
  • Mechanism: reward_final = r(x,y) - beta * KL(pi || pi_ref)
  • Effect: Without it, the model collapses to degenerate reward-maximizing behavior
  • Typical beta: 0.01 – 0.2, tuned on a per-task basis

Value Network (Critic)

  • Role: Estimates expected reward V(s) to compute advantages A = R - V(s)
  • Architecture: Same size as the policy model with a scalar value head
  • Cost: Roughly doubles GPU memory (two full model copies)
  • Training: Updated jointly with the policy via MSE on returns
PPO Challenges
  • Reward model quality is the ceiling — a noisy RM leads to reward hacking, not alignment
  • Training instability — requires careful tuning of learning rate, clipping epsilon, KL coefficient, and GAE lambda
  • High compute cost — four models in memory (policy, reference, RM, value network) during training
  • Sensitivity to hyperparameters — PPO is notoriously brittle; small changes can destabilize training
4.3

DPO & Offline Methods

Preference optimization without reinforcement learning

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) showed that you can skip the reward model and the online RL loop entirely. The key insight: the optimal policy under the RLHF objective can be expressed as a closed-form function of preference data. This collapses the three-stage RLHF pipeline into a single supervised learning step on preference pairs.

CORE IDEA

DPO Loss Function

DPO reparameterizes the reward into the policy directly. The implicit reward for any response is:

r(x, y) = beta * log( pi(y|x) / pi_ref(y|x) ) + const

The DPO loss maximizes the margin between preferred and rejected responses under this implicit reward:

L(DPO) = -log sigma( beta * [ log(pi(y_w|x)/pi_ref(y_w|x)) - log(pi(y_l|x)/pi_ref(y_l|x)) ] )

No reward model to train, no online generation, no value network. Just a supervised loss on preference pairs — as stable and cheap as SFT.

KTO

Kahneman-Tversky Optimization

Works with binary labels (good/bad) instead of preference pairs. Much easier to collect data — annotators just thumbs-up or thumbs-down individual responses, no need to compare pairs. Based on prospect theory: losses loom larger than gains, so the algorithm weights rejected examples more heavily.

ORPO

Odds Ratio Preference Optimization

Combines SFT and preference alignment in a single training step. The SFT loss teaches format while the odds ratio term simultaneously penalizes rejected responses. Eliminates the need for a separate SFT phase, reducing total training time and simplifying the pipeline.

SimPO

Simple Preference Optimization

A length-normalized, reference-free variant of DPO. By normalizing log-probabilities by sequence length, SimPO avoids the bias toward shorter responses that can plague DPO. Removing the reference model dependency means no need to keep pi_ref in memory during training. Often competitive with DPO while being simpler and cheaper to run.

Offline Methods — Pros vs Cons

Advantages:

  • Stable training — no RL instability, same as SFT
  • Cheap — no online generation, no reward model
  • Simple — standard supervised training loop
  • Reproducible — deterministic given fixed data

Limitations:

  • Limited to distribution of collected responses
  • Cannot discover novel behaviors beyond training data
  • Quality bounded by the annotation quality
  • No exploration — policy never generates and evaluates
4.4

GRPO Deep Dive

Group Relative Policy Optimization — eliminating the critic

GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) is the core online RL algorithm used by systems like DeepSeek-R1 and AWS Nova Forge RFT. It is a variant of policy optimization that eliminates the value network (critic) entirely by computing advantages relative to a group of sampled responses per prompt. This removes the memory overhead of maintaining a critic model while maintaining competitive alignment quality.

STEP-BY-STEP

GRPO Training Walkthrough

  1. Sample prompts — Draw a batch of prompts from the training dataset
  2. Generate N completions per prompt — The policy model generates N responses for each prompt (typically N = 2–16, controlled by number_generation)
  3. Score each completion — A reward function (Lambda, LLM judge, or remote endpoint) assigns a scalar reward to each response
  4. Compute group advantages — For each prompt, normalize rewards within the group:
advantage_i = ( reward_i - mean(rewards_group) ) / std(rewards_group)
  1. Policy gradient update — Compute the loss that increases probability of high-advantage responses:
L = -sum( advantage_i * log_prob(completion_i | prompt) )
  1. KL penalty — Add a KL divergence penalty against the reference policy to prevent reward hacking: L_total = L + beta * KL(pi || pi_ref)
  2. Optimizer step — AdamW updates model weights. Repeat from step 1.
GRPO vs PPO — Key Distinction

PPO requires a separate value network (critic) to estimate advantages, which doubles memory requirements and adds training complexity. GRPO eliminates the critic by computing advantages relative to the group of sampled responses for each prompt. This makes it more memory-efficient and simpler to implement, while maintaining similar alignment quality.

GRPO Advantages

  • No value network: ~50% less GPU memory vs PPO
  • Simpler training loop: No critic loss to balance
  • Self-normalizing: Group statistics provide automatic baselines
  • Proven at scale: Used by DeepSeek-R1, Nova Forge

GRPO Tradeoffs

  • Needs N ≥ 2 samples per prompt: Minimum for meaningful advantage estimation
  • Generation overhead: Must generate multiple completions per step
  • Variance with small groups: Advantage estimates are noisy when N is small
  • Reward function required: Still needs a way to score completions

4.5

Online vs Offline Alignment

Choosing the right alignment paradigm for your task

The most consequential decision in RL-based alignment is whether to use online methods (the model generates its own responses and learns from reward feedback) or offline methods (the model learns from pre-collected preference data without any generation). Each paradigm has distinct strengths, and modern practice often combines both.

ONLINE

GRPO / PPO

  • Model generates its own responses at each training step
  • Responses scored by reward function (Lambda, LLM judge, remote endpoint)
  • More compute-intensive — generation + scoring per step
  • Can discover novel behaviors beyond the training distribution
  • Best for verifiable tasks: code correctness, math accuracy, tool use, format compliance
OFFLINE

DPO / SimPO / KTO

  • Uses pre-collected preference pairs — no generation at training time
  • Training cost similar to SFT (same data pipeline, same loop)
  • Cheaper, more stable, highly reproducible
  • Limited to existing response quality distribution in the dataset
  • Best for subjective tasks: style, tone, safety, helpfulness

Industry Trend: Use DPO for an initial alignment pass (cheap, stable baseline), then GRPO for capability improvements on specific tasks where verifiable rewards exist. This two-phase approach gives you the stability of offline alignment plus the exploration power of online RL where it matters most.
4.6

Reward Design

The reward function defines what the model optimizes for — get it right or suffer the consequences

The reward function is the single most important design decision in RL-based alignment. A well-designed reward produces a helpful, capable model. A poorly designed reward produces a model that games the metric. There are three major paradigms for constructing rewards, each suited to different task types.

RLVR

RL via Verifiable Rewards

Rule-based reward functions for tasks with objective correctness criteria. The reward is computed deterministically — no learned model involved.

  • Code execution: Run code against test cases — pass = 1.0, fail = 0.0
  • Math verification: Extract final answer, compare against ground truth
  • Format compliance: Check JSON structure, field presence, type constraints
  • Regex matching: Verify output matches expected patterns

RLVR is the gold standard when applicable — deterministic, fast, no noise. The challenge is that most real-world tasks are not fully verifiable.

RLAIF

RL from AI Feedback

An LLM-as-judge evaluates responses against criteria (helpfulness, accuracy, safety). Scores are used as reward signals. Suitable for subjective quality where human preferences are hard to formalize. Trade-off: cheaper than human annotation but inherits the biases and blind spots of the judge model.

REMOTE

Remote Reward Functions

API-based reward computation where the customer runs evaluation in their own infrastructure. Enables complex evaluations: physics simulators, code with nested tool-calling, robotics tasks, multi-step web interaction. The training system sends (prompt, response) to a remote endpoint and receives a scalar reward.

Reward Hacking Mitigations
  • KL penalty: Constrains the policy from drifting too far from the reference, limiting exploitation of RM weaknesses
  • Reward model ensembles: Average rewards from multiple RMs — harder for the policy to simultaneously hack all of them
  • Constrained optimization: Set minimum thresholds on safety scores while maximizing helpfulness
  • Process reward models (PRMs): Reward each reasoning step, not just the final outcome — encourages correct reasoning rather than lucky guesses
  • Reward capping: Clip extreme reward values to prevent the policy from over-optimizing outliers
Multi-Objective Rewards — Constitutional AI

Real alignment requires balancing multiple objectives simultaneously. The Constitutional AI approach defines a weighted combination of helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty (the "HHH" criteria). In practice, the reward is often a weighted sum: R = w_h * R_helpful + w_s * R_safe + w_a * R_accurate. The weights encode the provider's alignment priorities and can be tuned per-deployment.

4.7

Multi-Turn RL & Agentic Training

Beyond single-turn: training models to use tools, plan, and recover from errors

Single-turn RL optimizes for one-shot question-answer quality. But modern LLM applications are increasingly agentic — the model must plan multi-step workflows, call external tools, interpret results, and recover from failures. Training for these capabilities requires RL environments that simulate real-world interaction patterns.

TOOL USE

RL for Tool-Calling

The model learns when and how to call tools through RL signal. Instead of SFT on tool-use demonstrations (which teaches imitation), RL lets the model discover optimal tool strategies:

  • When to call: Learn that some questions need a calculator, others don't
  • Parameter selection: Learn correct API parameters through trial-and-error
  • Result interpretation: Learn to extract relevant information from tool outputs
  • Fallback strategies: Learn to recover when a tool call fails or returns unexpected results
ENVIRONMENTS

Environment Design

  • Code sandbox: Isolated execution with test-case feedback
  • Web browsing: Simulated browser for navigation tasks
  • API interaction: Mock or real API endpoints for tool use
  • Multi-agent: Conversations with other LLMs or simulated users
CHALLENGES

Multi-Turn Difficulties

  • Credit assignment: Which step in a 10-step trajectory caused failure?
  • Sparse rewards: Only final outcome is scored — many steps get no signal
  • Environment reliability: External APIs may be flaky or rate-limited
  • Trajectory length: Long rollouts are expensive and slow to backprop through
BYOO Pattern — Bring Your Own Orchestrator

In production systems like AWS Nova Forge, customers run the RL environment in their own VPC infrastructure. The training system sends prompts and model responses to a customer-hosted endpoint, which orchestrates multi-turn conversations — tool calls, environment interaction, error handling — and returns aggregate reward scores. This keeps sensitive tools, data, and business logic within the customer's security boundary.

Modern RL Landscape

A taxonomy of alignment algorithms and where they are headed

The RL alignment landscape has evolved rapidly since InstructGPT. The trend is unmistakable: simpler algorithms are winning. The field has moved from the four-model complexity of PPO to methods that require only a policy and optionally a reference model. Here is the full landscape as of 2025–2026.

ONLINE WINNERS

GRPO Family

GRPO and its variants (DAPO, Dr. GRPO) have become the default for online RL. They eliminate the critic, simplify the training loop, and scale well. DAPO adds dynamic sampling (adjusting the number of generations based on task difficulty) and a clip-higher mechanism that prevents the policy from becoming overconfident on easy examples.

OFFLINE WINNERS

DPO / SimPO

DPO remains the dominant offline method for its simplicity and strong empirical results. SimPO is gaining ground as a drop-in replacement that removes reference model dependency and handles length bias. For teams with only binary feedback data, KTO is the go-to choice.

The Decision Framework

Choose Online RL (GRPO) When:

  • You have a verifiable reward (code tests, math answers)
  • You need the model to explore beyond existing data
  • Task performance is measurable and objective
  • You have sufficient compute budget for generation

Choose Offline (DPO/SimPO) When:

  • You have preference data (human or AI annotated)
  • The goal is style, tone, or safety alignment
  • You want maximum stability and reproducibility
  • Compute budget is limited
Where the Field Is Headed: The boundary between online and offline is blurring. Iterative DPO (generate → preference label → DPO → repeat) adds exploration to offline methods. Meanwhile, online methods are getting cheaper through efficient generation (speculative decoding in the RL loop) and better advantage estimation (token-level rewards in REINFORCE++). Expect convergence toward simple, scalable algorithms with optional exploration — the complexity of classic PPO is unlikely to return.
