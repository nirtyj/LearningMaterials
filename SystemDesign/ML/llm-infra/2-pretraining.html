2. Pre-Training at Scale

Next-token prediction, data pipelines, tokenization, scaling laws, and the plasticity-stability dilemma.

2.1

Pre-Training Paradigm

The deceptively simple objective behind modern LLMs

Pre-training is the foundation of every large language model. The core idea is elegantly simple: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. This single objective — causal language modeling (CLM) — is what turns raw text into a model that can reason, code, translate, and follow instructions.

Next-Token Prediction (Autoregressive LM)

At each position t in the sequence, the model predicts a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary, conditioned on all preceding tokens:

p(xt | x1, x2, …, xt−1 ; θ)

The training loss is the cross-entropy between the model’s predicted distribution and the one-hot ground truth at every position, averaged over all tokens in the batch:

L(θ) = − (1/T) ∑t=1..T log p(xt | x<t ; θ)
SELF-SUPERVISED

No human labels are needed. The text IS the label — each token serves as the target for the preceding context. This enables training on virtually unlimited internet text without any annotation cost.

SCALE

Modern pre-training runs process trillions of tokens across thousands of GPUs over months of continuous compute. Llama 3.1 405B trained on 15.6T tokens using 16,384 H100 GPUs.

Why Does This Simple Objective Work?

Predicting the next token is equivalent to lossless compression of the training data. To compress well, the model must build internal representations of syntax, semantics, world knowledge, reasoning chains, and even theory of mind. In the words of Ilya Sutskever: “Prediction is compression, and compression is understanding.” The model doesn’t just memorize — it learns transferable representations that generalize far beyond the training distribution.

All instruction-following, chat, and reasoning abilities are built ON TOP of this pre-trained foundation. Pre-training creates the knowledge; fine-tuning and RLHF shape the behavior.

2.2

Data Pipeline

From raw web crawl to training-ready token streams

Data quality is the single most impactful lever in pre-training. Models trained on cleaner, better-deduplicated data consistently outperform larger models trained on noisier corpora. The data pipeline is a multi-stage funnel that starts with petabytes of raw web crawl and produces a curated, tokenized training corpus.

Data Pipeline Overview Web Crawl (Common Crawl, ~250B pages) | v [Language ID] -----> Filter non-target languages | v [URL / Domain Filter] -> Block known low-quality domains | v [Heuristic Rules] ----> Length, repetition, boilerplate removal | v [Quality Classifier] -> Score text quality (high vs random web) | v [Deduplication] ------> Exact hash + MinHash/LSH fuzzy dedup | v [PII / Toxicity Filter] -> Regex PII removal + classifier | v [Tokenization] -------> BPE / SentencePiece encoding | v [Shuffling + Packing] -> Concat docs, shuffle, pack to seq_len | v Training-Ready Shards (.bin / .arrow files)

Deduplication

Deduplication is critical — it prevents memorization, reduces training FLOPs wasted on repeated content, and improves downstream quality. Two main approaches:

  • Exact dedup: Hash each document (SHA-256 or similar), remove exact duplicates. Fast but misses near-duplicates.
  • Fuzzy dedup (MinHash/LSH): Compute MinHash signatures, use Locality-Sensitive Hashing to find near-duplicates (e.g., Jaccard similarity > 0.8). Catches boilerplate templates, mirror sites, syndicated content.
  • Paragraph / n-gram dedup: Remove repeated paragraphs across documents. Used by FineWeb for finer granularity.

Lee et al. (2022) showed that deduplication alone can improve perplexity by 10%+ and significantly reduce memorization of training data.

Quality Filtering

  • Classifier-based: Train a fastText/BERT classifier on Wikipedia + curated books (positive) vs random web (negative). Keep pages with high quality score.
  • Perplexity filtering: Score with a small LM; remove very high or very low perplexity pages (gibberish or repetitive).
  • Heuristic rules: Min/max length, alphabet ratio, stopword density, line-level repetition, bullet-list ratio.

Safety Filtering

  • Toxicity classifiers: Trained detectors (e.g., Jigsaw, Perspective API) flag and remove toxic, hateful, or NSFW content.
  • PII removal: Regex patterns for emails, phone numbers, SSNs, credit cards. Named entity recognition for names and addresses.
  • CSAM filtering: Perceptual hashing + classifiers to detect and remove illegal content.

Major Open Datasets

Dataset Size Source Key Feature
Common Crawl ~250B pages Web crawl Raw foundation; requires heavy filtering
FineWeb (HuggingFace) 15T tokens Common Crawl Aggressive dedup + quality filtering; state-of-the-art open data
DCLM (DataComp-LM) 3T tokens Common Crawl Model-based quality scoring; competitive with FineWeb
RedPajama v2 30T tokens 84 CC snapshots Quality signals + dedup metadata included
The Pile 825 GB 22 curated sources Diverse: academic, code, books, StackExchange, USPTO
StarCoder Data 783 GB GitHub Permissively-licensed code in 80+ languages
Data Mix Ratios Matter Enormously

The blend of data domains — web text, books, code, academic papers, Wikipedia, conversations — has an outsized impact on model capabilities. Llama 2 used roughly: 89.7% web, 4.5% code, 2.5% Wikipedia, 2.5% books, 0.8% academic. Increasing the code ratio (Llama 3 used ~17% code) dramatically improves reasoning and math abilities even on non-code tasks. These ratios are typically the most closely guarded secrets of frontier labs.

2.3

Tokenization

Converting text to numbers the neural network can process

Neural networks operate on numbers, not characters. Tokenization is the process of converting raw text into a sequence of integer token IDs from a fixed vocabulary. The choice of tokenizer affects sequence length, model efficiency, multilingual capability, and even reasoning performance.

Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

The most common algorithm. BPE starts with individual bytes (or characters) and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pair into a new token, repeating until the desired vocabulary size is reached.

BPE Training Example Input corpus: "low low low low lower lower newest newest" Step 0: Characters → l o w _ l o w e r _ n e w e s t Step 1: Merge (l,o) → lo w _ lo w e r _ n e w e s t Step 2: Merge (lo,w) → low _ low e r _ n e w e s t Step 3: Merge (e,s) → low _ low e r _ n e w es t Step 4: Merge (es,t) → low _ low e r _ n e w est ...continue until vocab_size reached

Used by GPT-2/3/4, Llama 3, Mistral. Llama 3 uses a 128K BPE vocabulary trained with tiktoken.

SentencePiece

A language-agnostic tokenizer that operates directly on raw Unicode bytes, treating the input as a stream of bytes rather than pre-tokenized words. It can use BPE or Unigram algorithms internally. Because it doesn’t assume whitespace-delimited words, it handles CJK languages, code, and multilingual text gracefully.

Used by Llama 1–2, Gemma, T5, mT5. Llama 2 uses a 32K SentencePiece vocabulary.

Vocabulary Size Tradeoffs

Model Vocab Size Tokenizer Notes
GPT-2 50,257 BPE Byte-level BPE, English-centric
Llama 2 32,000 SentencePiece Compact vocab, longer sequences
Llama 3 128,256 tiktoken BPE 4x larger vocab → ~15% shorter sequences
Gemma 2 256,000 SentencePiece Massive vocab for multilingual coverage
GPT-4 ~100,000 tiktoken BPE Optimized for code + multilingual
Larger Vocab Advantages
  • Shorter token sequences (fewer forward passes for same text)
  • Better multilingual and code coverage
  • Less byte-level fallback needed
Larger Vocab Costs
  • Larger embedding table (vocab_size × d_model)
  • More memory for the LM head (output projection)
  • Rare tokens may be poorly trained

Special Tokens & Chat Templates

Beyond content tokens, tokenizers define special tokens that signal structure:

<bos> Begin of sequence — signals the start of a new document/conversation <eos> End of sequence — signals completion; used for stopping generation <pad> Padding — fills shorter sequences in a batch to uniform length Chat template example (Llama 3): <|begin_of_text|><|start_header_id|>system<|end_header_id|> You are a helpful assistant.<|eot_id|> <|start_header_id|>user<|end_header_id|> What is 2+2?<|eot_id|> <|start_header_id|>assistant<|end_header_id|>
Tokenization Failure Modes

BPE tokenizers trained primarily on English text can produce extremely long token sequences for non-English languages (sometimes 3–5x more tokens for the same semantic content in Hindi, Arabic, or CJK). This means non-English users pay more in API costs and hit context limits sooner. Code and math expressions can also tokenize poorly — a single variable name like getElementById might become 3–4 tokens. Byte-level fallback (encoding unknown characters as raw UTF-8 bytes) ensures nothing is ever out-of-vocabulary, but at a severe efficiency cost.

2.4

Training Phases & Scheduling

Learning rate schedules, mixed precision, and the rhythm of large-scale training

Pre-training is not a single monolithic phase. Modern training runs are carefully orchestrated with learning rate schedules, batch size ramps, mixed precision strategies, and checkpointing protocols that keep the run stable across months of continuous compute.

Multi-Phase Training

Large-scale pre-training typically follows three phases:

Training Phases Learning Rate ^ | peak_lr | /--------\ | / \ Annealing | / Main \ (higher quality data, | / Pre-Training \ smaller LR) | / (bulk of tokens) \ /--------\ |/ Warmup \ / Cooldown \ +------------------------\-/-------------\-------> 0 80% tokens 90% 100% Steps Phase 1: Main Pre-Training — Bulk of compute, general web data Phase 2: Annealing — Higher quality data mix, decaying LR Phase 3: Cooldown — Final LR decay to near-zero min_lr

Learning Rate Schedule

The standard schedule has three stages:

  • Warmup (linear ramp): Gradually increase LR from ~0 to peak over 1–2K steps. Prevents large, unstable gradient updates when weights are randomly initialized.
  • Peak → Cosine Decay: After warmup, follow a cosine schedule from peak_lr down to min_lr (typically 10% of peak). This is the main training phase.
  • Min LR: The floor learning rate, maintained until end of training. Too low and the model stops learning; too high and loss becomes noisy.
lr(t) = min_lr + 0.5 × (peak_lr − min_lr) × (1 + cos(π × t / T))

Typical values: peak_lr = 3e-4 (for 7B models), min_lr = 3e-5, warmup = 2000 steps.

Batch Size Ramp

Start with a small batch size (e.g., 0.5M tokens) and gradually increase to the full batch size (e.g., 4M tokens) over the first few thousand steps. Small batches early on provide noisier but more diverse gradient signal, helping the model escape poor local minima. Larger batches later provide stable, efficient gradient estimates.

Gradient Clipping

Clip the global gradient norm to prevent exploding gradients that can destabilize training or cause loss spikes. The standard approach clips to max_norm = 1.0:

if ||g|| > max_norm: g ← g × max_norm / ||g||

Mixed Precision Training

Modern LLM training uses multiple numeric precisions simultaneously to balance speed, memory, and numerical stability:

Component Precision Why
Forward / backward pass BF16 2x throughput, same dynamic range as FP32, no loss scaling needed
Master weights FP32 Small weight updates (LR × gradient) need FP32 precision to accumulate
Optimizer states FP32 Adam momentum/variance require high precision for stability
Gradient communication BF16 or FP16 Reduces all-reduce bandwidth by 2x

BF16 has largely replaced FP16 for LLM training because it has the same exponent range as FP32 (8 bits), eliminating the need for loss scaling. FP16 requires careful loss scaling to avoid underflow in gradients.

Checkpointing Strategy

Save full model + optimizer state every N steps (typically every 500–1000 steps). Keep a rolling window of the last 3–5 checkpoints to manage storage. Checkpoints are critical for recovery from hardware failures (which are inevitable at scale — Meta reported ~100 failures during Llama 3 training) and for branching experiments (e.g., trying different annealing data mixes from the same checkpoint). Async checkpointing avoids blocking training.

2.5

Scaling Laws

How loss, compute, data, and parameters interact — and what this means for model design

Scaling laws are the empirical relationships that let us predict model performance before training. They have fundamentally changed how labs plan training runs, allocate budgets, and choose model sizes.

Kaplan et al. (2020) — OpenAI Scaling Laws

The first comprehensive study showed that loss scales as a power law with three variables: compute (C), dataset size (D), and model parameters (N):

L(N) ∼ N−0.076   |   L(D) ∼ D−0.095   |   L(C) ∼ C−0.050

Key insight: loss improves smoothly and predictably with scale. There are no sharp phase transitions — you can extrapolate from small runs to predict large model performance. The original recommendation was to scale model size faster than data size.

Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al. 2022) — Compute-Optimal Training

DeepMind’s landmark result overturned the “scale parameters first” wisdom. For a fixed compute budget, the compute-optimal strategy is to scale parameters and tokens roughly equally:

Optimal tokens ≈ 20 × parameters

This meant GPT-3 (175B params, 300B tokens) was massively undertrained — it should have seen ~3.5T tokens. Chinchilla (70B params, 1.4T tokens) matched GPT-3’s performance with 4x fewer parameters.

Chinchilla-Optimal Configurations

Compute Budget Optimal Params Optimal Tokens Token:Param Ratio
1019 FLOPs ~400M ~8B 20:1
1020 FLOPs ~1B ~20B 20:1
1021 FLOPs ~3B ~60B 20:1
1022 FLOPs ~10B ~200B 20:1
1023 FLOPs ~30B ~600B 20:1
1024 FLOPs ~100B ~2T 20:1
Post-Chinchilla: The Llama Approach — Over-Training for Inference

Chinchilla optimizes for training compute. But in practice, you train once and serve millions of times. Meta’s key insight: over-train smaller models far beyond the Chinchilla-optimal point because a smaller model is dramatically cheaper to serve.

  • Llama 1 7B: trained on 1T tokens (Chinchilla-optimal would be ~140B tokens — 7x over-trained)
  • Llama 2 7B: trained on 2T tokens (~14x over-trained)
  • Llama 3 8B: trained on 15T tokens (~100x over-trained!)

The loss curves show diminishing returns from over-training, but the inference cost savings are enormous. Serving a 7B model is ~50x cheaper per token than serving a 70B model. The extra training compute pays for itself quickly at scale.

The Key Tradeoff: Training compute is a one-time cost; inference compute is ongoing. For models that will be served at scale, over-training a smaller model is almost always the right call. This is why Llama-class models (7–13B) trained on massive token counts have become the industry standard for deployment.

Scaling laws enable “scaling law experiments” — train several small models at different scales, fit the power law, and predict the performance of a much larger target model before committing the full compute budget.

2.6

Continued Pre-Training (CPT)

Domain adaptation through further pre-training on specialized corpora

Continued Pre-Training (CPT) takes an existing pre-trained model and further trains it on domain-specific text (medical, legal, code, finance) using the same next-token prediction objective. The goal is to inject deep domain knowledge into the model’s weights — knowledge that goes beyond what retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) can provide.

When to Use CPT vs RAG vs SFT

Approach Use When Knowledge Type Compute Cost
CPT Model lacks fundamental domain knowledge Deep, parametric (in weights) High (days–weeks of GPU)
RAG Knowledge is factual, changes frequently Shallow, non-parametric (retrieved) Low (no training)
SFT Model has knowledge but wrong behavior/format Behavioral (how to respond) Medium (hours–days)
CPT Best Practices
  • Mix domain + general data: Use 5–20% general replay data to prevent catastrophic forgetting
  • Lower learning rate: Use 10–50% of the original pre-training peak LR
  • LR re-warmup: Brief warmup to the new (lower) peak, then cosine decay
  • Monitor general benchmarks: Track MMLU, HellaSwag alongside domain evals
  • Start from base model: NOT the instruct/chat variant (loses instruction tuning)
CPT Risks
  • Catastrophic forgetting: Model loses general capabilities if trained only on narrow data
  • Overfitting: Domain corpora are often small; easy to memorize
  • Lost instruction following: After CPT, the model needs SFT again to regain chat capabilities
  • Expensive: Requires significant compute even with LoRA

Notable CPT Examples

Model Base Domain CPT Data Key Result
Code Llama Llama 2 Code 500B code tokens 62% HumanEval (vs 30% base)
BioMistral Mistral 7B Biomedical PubMed Central +8% on medical QA benchmarks
SaulLM Mistral 7B Legal 30B legal tokens SOTA on legal benchmarks
FinMA Llama Finance Financial filings + news Strong on FinBench tasks

The typical full pipeline is: Base Model → CPT (domain knowledge) → SFT (instruction following) → RLHF/DPO (alignment). Each stage builds on the previous one.

2.7

Plasticity-Stability Dilemma

The fundamental tension in continual learning: learn new things without forgetting old ones

The plasticity-stability dilemma is the central challenge of continued pre-training and any form of continual learning. A model must be plastic enough to learn new tasks and absorb new domain knowledge, yet stable enough to retain the general capabilities it already has. Train too aggressively on new data and the model forgets how to do basic reasoning; train too conservatively and it never truly learns the new domain.

Three Pillars of Anti-Forgetting

The industry addresses the plasticity-stability dilemma through three complementary pillars:

PILLAR 1

Data-Based Strategies (Replay)

  • Data Mixing (Experience Replay): Mix 5–20% original general pre-training data with new domain data. This maintains coverage of the original training distribution, preventing gradient drift away from the general-capability manifold.
  • Self-Synthesized Rehearsal (SSR): When original pre-training data is unavailable (common for proprietary base models), use the model itself to generate “old-domain” data for replay. The model becomes its own memory.
  • Data Mixing Laws: Mathematical scaling laws that predict the optimal new-vs-old data ratio to prevent drops on benchmarks like MMLU and GSM8K. These help choose mixing ratios without expensive grid search.
PILLAR 2

Regularization (Constraining Weights)

  • Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC): The Fisher information matrix identifies which weights are critical for previously learned tasks. A penalty term is added to the loss to discourage modifying these important weights during new training:
LEWC = Lnew + (λ/2) ∑i Fi (θi − θi*)2
  • Knowledge Distillation: A frozen copy of the original model (Teacher) constrains the new model (Student) from drifting. The student’s outputs must stay close to the teacher’s on general-domain inputs, adding a KL-divergence loss term.
  • Mixout: Randomly resets some weights to their original pre-trained values during training, acting as a stochastic anchor to the base model. Similar in spirit to Dropout but resets to pre-trained values instead of zero.
PILLAR 3

Optimization & Architecture

  • Learning Rate Re-warming & Re-decay: Start with a very low LR that slowly increases, matching the model’s existing optimization momentum, then decays. This avoids “shocking” the model with large early gradient steps that overwrite existing knowledge.
  • PEFT / LoRA: Freeze core weights entirely and only train small low-rank adapter matrices. This makes it physically impossible to erase base knowledge since the original parameters remain untouched.
  • Layer-wise Learning Rates: Assign very low LR to bottom layers (foundational skills like syntax, grammar, basic reasoning) and higher LR to top layers (task-specific specialization). Bottom layers encode universal linguistic knowledge that should be preserved.
Industry Recipe Summary — Preventing Forgetting
  1. Architecture: Freeze bottom layers or use LoRA to protect foundational knowledge
  2. Data: Mix in ~10% general pre-training data to maintain distribution coverage
  3. Optimization: Use LR re-warming to prevent weight shocks at the start of training
  4. Evaluation: Monitor continuously with forgetting metrics like Backward Transfer (BWT) — measures the change in performance on old tasks after learning new ones
Backward Transfer (BWT) Metric

BWT quantifies how much a model forgets previously learned tasks after training on new ones. It is defined as the average change in performance on all prior tasks after new training:

BWT = (1 / (T−1)) ∑i=1..T−1 (RT,i − Ri,i)

Where RT,i is the performance on task i after training on task T, and Ri,i is the performance on task i right after training on it. BWT = 0 means no forgetting. BWT < 0 means the model has forgotten (negative backward transfer). BWT > 0 (rare) means new training actually improved old-task performance. In practice, aim for BWT > −2% on critical benchmarks.

Practical Decision Framework

  • Small domain shift, limited data (<1B tokens): Use LoRA/QLoRA — safest against forgetting
  • Moderate domain shift, good data (1–50B tokens): Full CPT with 10–20% replay + LR re-warmup
  • Large domain shift, massive data (>50B tokens): Full CPT with replay + EWC regularization + distillation
  • No original data available: Use SSR (self-synthesized rehearsal) + LoRA as safety net

There is no single “best” technique — the optimal strategy depends on the domain gap, data availability, compute budget, and acceptable forgetting threshold. In practice, teams combine multiple pillars (e.g., replay + low LR + LoRA) for the best results.
