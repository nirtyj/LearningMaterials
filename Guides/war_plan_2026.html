Executive TL;DR

Three things changed in 2025–2026 that you must internalize before reading anything else:

  1. Meta's loop changed more between Oct 2025 and Q1 2026 than in the previous five years. E7/M1 candidates now get only one coding round and it is the 60-minute AI-assisted CoderPad round (multi-file project with Claude Haiku 3.5 / Llama 4 Maverick / GPT-4o-mini sidebar). E6 gets one standard + one AI-assisted. Below E6 it's two standard + one optional AI-assisted. Treat this as the single highest-leverage prep delta — most prep docs people pass around are already obsolete.
  2. Anthropic's bar is values-fit, not LeetCode. The CodeSignal 4-level practical (banking ledger, in-memory KV with TTL/persistence, web-crawler with concurrency) is widely leaked. Anthropic recruiters have told interviewing.io explicitly that the values/culture round is where most candidates fail.
  3. Frontier labs and robotics shops weight a "statement of exceptional work" / project deep-dive presentation more than coding. xAI literally requires a written exceptional-work statement on the application; OpenAI and Pi do project deep-dives; Anthropic asks for a 5-hour take-home + reference calls. Your portfolio (manufacturing AI / FactoryAI / GUI agents / VLA / robotics distributed-systems work) is your single best asset. Polish it before you grind LeetCode.

Your starting position. Amazon L6 with a productionized physical-AI flagship: NVIDIA lighthouse, AWS blog case study, 99% defect detection, 95% pick-and-place, GUI-Owl-1.5 ADB agent on Fire. A credible Nimble offer in the recent past. Floor is $700K–$900K Y1. Ceiling, with execution, is $1.5M+ Y1 at xAI / Anthropic / Figure / Google L7.

Lane 1 Amazon — L7 Principal SDE

Loop Structure (verified 2025–2026)
  • Recruiter screen (30–45 min): role/level/location/comp/story.
  • Hiring Manager call (1 hr): increasingly behavioral-heavy at L7 — flagged as "one of the most unique I ever experienced" by Pragmatic Engineer.
  • Technical phone screen (1 round, coding + LP-laced).
  • The Loop (5–6 rounds, ~1 hr each): 2× system design (yes, L7 gets two; one is "deep architecture / write-up"), 1× Bar Raiser (pure LP, has veto), 1× hiring manager deep-dive, 1× coding/technical (often a single high-bar problem with multiple extensions), 1× peer/cross-functional / org-design.
  • Many L7 loops include a written exercise (6-pager / design doc) submitted ahead of onsite — confirmed by Bar Raisers and Scarlet Ink's Dave Anderson posts. Format = Amazon narrative (no bullets, "press release / FAQ" or pure narrative). Read Bryar/Carr's Working Backwards; write 3 practice 6-pagers.
What the Bar Raiser Actually Scores at L7
  • "Is this person helping a manager solve the manager's problems? Are they helping the manager be out of the manager's job? Are they driving the team? Can they conduct meetings proactively? Can they solve problems in ambiguous areas?"
  • Cross-functional reach + scope — how many cross-functional teams influenced and how big the influence was. The L7-specific question: "What's the level of leadership you've presented to?"
  • Disagreements must be across or up the org chart, not down. "Only disagreeing across or down" is a documented L7 anti-pattern.
Rejection Modes (~25% of technically-passing SDEs hit these)
  • Negative on Earn Trust / Have Backbone — Disagree and Commit = blow-up even if everything else is positive.
  • Mentioning revenue or cost savings under $1M is an automatic red flag; under $5M is suspect at L7.
  • Speaking about a manual process without proactively saying why you didn't automate it.
  • Over-reliance on STAR. STAR is a filter, not a script; memorized STAR responses are a fail signal.
  • "We" instead of "I" — Bar Raiser drills until they find the hole.
  • 3-min monologues. Bar Raisers want 15–30 sec opening + drill-down.
  • Trying to guess which LP is being tested. Just answer the question asked.
  • Internal-promotion mindset to L7 — they want scope you don't yet have, not what you currently own.
LPs That Matter Most at L7 SDE

Ownership, Deliver Results, Invent and Simplify, Insist on the Highest Standards, Think Big, Hire and Develop the Best, Earn Trust. Your manufacturing-AI / FactoryAI / robotics scope maps cleanly to Think Big and Invent and Simplify — overweight those.

Recent Question Leaks
  • System design: "Design a multi-tenant LLM inference fleet for an AWS service customer with SLA tiers" (Blind, Mar 2026).
  • "Design Amazon's next-gen warehouse robotics fleet orchestration" (FactoryAI/robotics relevant).
  • Coding: design-heavy — LRU/TTL cache with snapshots, in-memory KV with versioned reads. L7 doesn't typically get raw LeetCode hards; "design + implement + extend three times."
  • Behavioral: "Tell me about a technical decision you made that affected 3+ orgs and was reversed by a higher-up" (Disagree-and-Commit / Earn Trust trap).
Offer-Getter Specifics

Lane 1 Meta — E7 Senior Staff (most-changed loop in 2026)

2026 Loop Structure
  • Recruiter screen.
  • Leadership Assessment (replaces phone screen for E6+, half behavioral / half coding). Gating — hiring panel decides here whether you survive the rest.
  • Onsite (E7+ / M1):
    • 1× AI-assisted coding round (60 min) — the only coding round in the loop.
    • 2× system design (architecture + product/design split — the "core puzzle" deep-dive decides E6 vs E7).
    • 1× technical retrospective (project deep-dive — where E7 vs E6 leveling is decided).
    • 1× cross-functional partnership.
    • 1× leadership/people (sometimes called "behavioral" — E7 weighting heavier than E6).

  • Team matching is before the loop for E7 (vs after for E6) — the #1 source of multi-month delays at Meta in 2025–2026.
The AI-Assisted Coding Round — Every Public Detail
  • 60 minutes in CoderPad with three-panel layout (file explorer / code editor / AI chat + problem instructions).
  • Models in pad: GPT-4o mini, Claude Haiku 3.5/4.5, Llama 4 Maverick (most often confirmed); GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4/4.5, Gemini 2.5 Pro listed as supported but rarely confirmed in real interviews.
  • AI cannot edit your files — chat-only. You paste/write code yourself.
  • One thematic question with multiple checkpoints (3–4 stages): clearing 3 checkpoints is the minimum signal; aim for 4+. Substantial scope: ~120+ LOC.
  • Three observed scenarios: (1) build feature into existing structure, (2) extend partial implementation, (3) debug broken implementation.
  • Real leaked problems: BFS maze with directional gates (>/< controlling traversal), maximizing unique characters across word sets, fixing 2-of-4 broken unit tests in utils.py then implementing solve.py, Filesystem Snapshot Diff, Chatroom Sanitization (regex/string), Maze + Portals/Locked Doors, Wordle Solver (practice env).
  • The trap: "Claude Sonnet worked brilliantly in practice but gave wrong answers repeatedly during the interview" on a maze problem. Multiple candidates report the in-interview AI is significantly less helpful than the practice environment AI.
  • Meta's official line: "AI use is optional." Reality: it's not entirely optional — using AI well is a productivity multiplier on the boilerplate but a footgun on logic.
  • Always ask your recruiter for the practice CoderPad with the AI tab — the sample problem ("the puzzle") is invaluable. Candidates who skip it consistently underperform.
System Design at E7 (Stefan Mai, 1000+ interviews)
  • Don't over-explain basics. "If it's part of the 101 curriculum, don't explain it unless prompted. Talk to your interviewer like a more experienced peer."
  • Talk like a peer; identify the hard parts and gravitate there. "Great staff-level engineers know intuitively which parts are straightforward and gravitate towards the most difficult parts."
  • Make decisions, don't list options. "Well, which one would you choose?"
  • Memorize non-functional requirements as a checklist: availability 99.99%, scalability up/down, security, durability 10 yrs, latency p95 200ms, HA-vs-strong-consistency. Just shoot them.
  • Pacing benchmark for the 35-min E6/E7 design: clarify+nonfunctional in 10 min, high-level + API + schema + core puzzle in 20 min, deep dives in last 5 min.
E7 Leveling Rubric

"2 coding (LC medium/hard), 2 system design, 1 cross-functional partnership, 1 technical retrospective, 1 other" (Blind from confirmed E7 hire). For tech retrospective: "Do you have examples where your work changed the company direction? Did you invent an industry standard?"

Common E7 Rejection Reasons
  • Behavioral round downlevels you to E6 even if technical is solid.
  • "Survive at E7" challenge: existing E7s are supposed to know very deep in the stack and lead people in a big way. Tech retrospective must show org-wide impact, not deep-stack-only.
  • Pasting AI output unread; treating the round as a prompt-engineering interview; skipping running tests after each checkpoint; not explaining WHY you accepted/rejected an AI suggestion in real time.
Cross-Functional Partnership Round at E7

Interviewer is often an EM or PM-equivalent. Expect: "Tell me about a time a senior partner challenged your technical decision in a public forum" — show you didn't escalate emotionally and resolved through async data + 1:1 alignment ("nemawashi"-style). Lead through influence, not authority.

  1. Don't over-explain basics. Talk to the interviewer like a more experienced peer.
  2. Solve simple flavors of problems simply. ("10,000 places, updated infrequently. Staff candidate: I'll just keep them in memory; geo-index isn't needed at this scale.")

Negotiation-Stage Interview Talk Prep

Comp negotiation tactics are covered separately; this section covers the conversational interview moments at the negotiation stage.

The "Manager Intent" Call After the Loop

  • Standard at Meta (post-loop "team match"), Snowflake (specific team selection at Principal), Databricks, Anthropic (sometimes the hiring manager joins references stage). At Google, this can come as part of team matching after hiring committee approval.
  • What it tests: whether you have specific opinions about what you want to work on; whether you'll take ownership immediately; whether you're picking the company or the team.
  • Prep: arrive with 2–3 specific projects/teams you've researched at the company. Reference recent published work (Anthropic Constitutional AI, OpenAI Preparedness, NVIDIA GR00T N2, Snowflake Cortex AI, Databricks Mosaic, Meta Llama 4). Have 1 strong opinion about what the right next bet is in your domain.
  • Trap: passivity ("I'd be open to anything") — at Staff+ this is read as low-conviction. Take a stand.

Reference Check Prep

  • Standard at Anthropic, OpenAI, Snowflake (heavily), Databricks (heavily), π. Your references will be called and asked structured questions mapped to the company's leveling rubric.
  • Prep: warm 5 references in advance (former managers, peers, direct reports). Send each a 1-page brief with: your target role, the company's level expectations, the 2–3 stories you've been telling in interviews, the specific traits you want them to validate.
  • Anthropic specifically: written reference feedback + sometimes calls. Request your references write 4–6 sentences in advance covering values fit + intellectual honesty + how you handle ambiguity + a specific concrete example.
  • Trap: a reference who paints a generic glowing picture is worse than one who acknowledges a real growth area + names how you addressed it. Calibrate references to be honest.

Final-Stage Skip-Level / Executive Chats

  • Anthropic: final chat sometimes with director or in rare cases Dario Amodei. Context: cultural fit at the mission level. Have a thesis on AI safety priorities, an honest disagreement with their public position, and a personal story of why this matters to you. Do not pitch yourself. It's a values conversation; pitching reads as misaligned.
  • xAI: chats with Igor Babuschkin / similar. Topic: where Grok / Imagine should go technically. Have a take on data-flywheel strategy, post-training quality, multi-modal tradeoffs. Be ready for combative styles — answer with conviction, don't placate.
  • Physical Intelligence: final chat with Karol Hausman or Sergey Levine. Topic: VLA technical strategy. Have a thesis on why π's flow-matching policy approach is right (or where you'd push back). Discuss data scarcity, embodiment transfer, real-time inference tradeoffs.
  • Figure: chat with Brett Adcock common. Topic: humanoid commercialization velocity. Cultural fit signal: ship-fast urgency + manufacturing-aware engineering. Your manufacturing AI background is on-thesis.
  • Snowflake / Databricks: VP Engineering or Director final. Topic: scope, scale, what you'd own in year 1.

The "Why Are You Leaving Amazon" Question — Universal Handling

You will get this in every loop. The mistake is bashing Amazon (reads as low EQ, low Earn-Trust signal even if the company isn't Amazon). The other mistake is being vague.

Template (adjust per audience):

  • Frame the move forward, not backward. "I've spent 14 years at Amazon shipping manufacturing AI / robotics / GUI-agents — the next problem I want to solve is [specific thing this company does]. Amazon's manufacturing AI scope is incredible but the [VLA / agentic / frontier] frontier is happening at [target company] and I want to be in the room where that's decided."
  • At Anthropic: add the safety/mission angle honestly. "The same things that drew me to scaled-systems work at Amazon — risk management, reliability under uncertainty — are what I think safety engineering at the frontier needs."
  • At π / Figure / 1X: emphasize the physical-AI / embodied transition. "Manufacturing AI taught me that the gap between simulation and real-world deployment is where the actual problems live. That's the entire game in humanoid/VLA right now."
  • At Meta / Google: emphasize the scale and influence delta. "L7-level scope at the company inventing the next generation of [Llama / Gemini / agentic systems] is where I want my next 5 years."
  • At Microsoft: emphasize platform leverage. Avoid the trap of comparing M$ vs Amazon culture; talk about Azure AI / Copilot platform reach.
  • At xAI: emphasize speed and frontier ambition. "The compounding rate of capability per engineer at xAI is unmatched."

The meta-rule: every "why leaving Amazon" answer must end with a forward-looking specific (a team, a product, a research direction). Never end on a complaint.

The "What Would You Do in Your First 90 Days" Question

  • Almost universal at Staff+/Principal final rounds.
  • Structure: (a) ramp-up plan (paired with an existing engineer, read X codebases, talk to Y stakeholders) — week 1–4. (b) first quick-win you can identify from the JD — week 4–8. (c) propose a 6–12 month strategic bet you'd validate — week 8–12.
  • Trap: proposing big rewrites or org changes day 1. This reads as missing humility. Frame the strategic bet as "I'd want to validate by talking to X, Y, Z first."

The "What Questions Do You Have for Me" Close

At Staff+, having specific, well-researched questions is part of the rubric. Have 3 ready per interviewer type:

  • For peer engineer: "What's the most painful operational issue your team hit in the last 6 months and how was it resolved?" / "Where does the team disagree internally on technical direction?"
  • For hiring manager: "What does someone need to do in their first year to be considered to have raised the bar at this level?" / "What's the role's biggest risk of failure in your view?"
  • For exec / skip-level: "What's the bet this company is making that you're not yet sure will pay off?" / "What's the most contrarian belief held internally?"
  • Trap: asking questions you could have Googled (comp, benefits, basic process). Burns credibility.

The "Honest Concerns" Moment

  • Frontier labs (Anthropic specifically; sometimes OpenAI) explicitly create space for you to express what concerns you about working there. They're testing whether you'll be honest under hiring incentive pressure.
  • Have 2 real concerns ready: e.g., "the deployment cadence vs safety mission tension," "compensation cliff if equity doesn't pan out," "the intensity of the work culture given my life situation," "scope ambiguity at a fast-growing company."
  • Answer honestly. Then explain why you're moving forward despite the concerns.
  • Trap: "I have no concerns" — this is automatically a fail at Anthropic.
